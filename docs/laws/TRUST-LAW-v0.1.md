# TRUST LAW v0.1
## Operational Trust Accumulation Rules For Governed Infrastructure

---

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the immutable trust law governing operational trust
accumulation, legitimacy boundaries, trust invalidation, and evidence-based
dependency formation across the governed cognition stack.

It standardizes:

- trust accumulation
- trust invalidation
- trust boundaries
- trust continuity
- trust replayability
- trust evidence requirements
- trust failure semantics
- dependency legitimacy
- operational credibility formation

It does not redesign runtime semantics, replay semantics, workflow semantics,
authority semantics, or validation semantics. Existing canon is binding. Trust
law sits adjacent to those laws and governs how their joint output becomes
admissible as a basis for operational dependency.

---

## 2. Why Trust Law Matters

Operational dependency without trust law degrades into belief. Belief is not
an admissibility primitive. Branding is not an admissibility primitive.
Capability is not an admissibility primitive. Adoption is not an admissibility
primitive.

A system that requests trust by assertion is asking to be relied upon for
reasons that cannot be reconstructed from the record. A system that
accumulates operational credibility through replayable continuity is being
relied upon for reasons that can.

Trust law exists to convert operational dependency from belief into
reconstructable evidence.

---

## 3. Trust As Operational Accumulation

Operational trust is not granted by assertion, branding, intelligence,
capability, or adoption. Operational trust accumulates only through bounded,
replayable, validated continuity under pressure across time.

Trust is therefore a slow-moving artifact. It cannot be issued. It cannot be
purchased. It cannot be inherited. It cannot be transferred by association.
It can only be accumulated by the same system, against the same canon, across
time, under recorded pressure.

A system whose trust appears suddenly has not accumulated trust. It has
accumulated reputation, which is a separate and inadmissible quantity.

---

## 4. Trust And Replay

Replay law defines how operational history is reconstructed. Trust law depends
on that reconstruction directly: an operational claim is trustworthy only if
the record that produced it can be replayed to the same outcome.

A claim that cannot be replayed against its record is not an operational
claim. It is testimony. Trust does not accumulate against testimony.

Replay does not produce trust on its own. Replay produces the substrate on
which trust is permitted to accumulate.

---

## 5. Trust And Validation

Validation law defines how operational decisions become mechanically
inspectable. Trust law depends on that inspectability: a validated outcome
contributes to operational credibility only when its validation artifact is
itself reconstructable under validation law.

An outcome that passes validation but whose validation cannot be replayed is
not a trust-accumulating event. It is an unverifiable assertion of an
unverifiable assertion.

Trust accumulates only behind a validation surface that holds.

---

## 6. Trust And Authority

Authority law defines the bounded scope within which governed action is
legitimate. Trust law depends on that boundary: trust accumulated under one
declared scope of authority does not transfer to surfaces outside that scope.

A system that quietly extends its operational authority does not extend its
trust. It invalidates the trust accumulated under the prior boundary, because
the boundary itself was load-bearing for that trust.

Authority expansion without explicit, recorded transition is a trust failure,
not a capability gain.

---

## 7. Trust And Workflow Continuity

Workflow law defines how governed operations compose across time. Trust law
depends on that composition: trust accumulates against continuous workflow
records, not against isolated successful events.

A successful event in a discontinuous workflow record contributes nothing to
operational trust. The continuity is the evidence; the event alone is not.

Trust is therefore a property of records over time, not a property of moments.

---

## 8. Immutable Trust Principles

The following principles are invariant. They do not bend to implementation
convenience, market pressure, adoption pressure, organizational preference,
or operator urgency.

1. Operational trust is accumulated, not granted.
2. Operational trust is bounded by evidence.
3. Operational trust is replayable or it is not trust.
4. Operational trust is validated mechanically or it is not trust.
5. Operational trust is reconstructable from the record or it is not trust.
6. Operational trust is explicit about unsupported surfaces.
7. Operational trust is explicit about failure boundaries.
8. Operational trust is pressure-tested across time.
9. Operational trust does not transfer across boundaries it has not crossed.
10. Operational trust resets when its substrate is invalidated.

A claim of trust that violates any of these principles is not an operational
trust claim. It is a marketing claim, and marketing claims are not admissible.

---

## 9. Operational Trust Requirements

An operational trust position is legitimate only when all of the following
hold:

- the underlying records are replayable
- the underlying validation artifacts are reconstructable
- the declared authority scope is intact
- the workflow continuity is intact
- the unsupported surfaces are explicit
- the failure boundaries are explicit
- the accumulation interval is recorded
- the pressure under which accumulation occurred is recorded

Partial satisfaction does not yield partial trust. A trust position is either
reconstructable from the record under these requirements, or it is not.

---

## 10. Dependency Formation Rules

Operational dependency on a governed system must form against evidence, not
against assertion. A dependency is legitimate only when it forms against:

- replayable releases
- reconstructable validation artifacts
- declared authority scope
- declared unsupported surfaces
- declared failure boundaries
- a recorded accumulation interval

A dependency formed without those references is a belief in a system, not a
dependency on it. Belief-based dependency is operationally indistinguishable
from luck.

---

## 11. Trust Accumulation Rules

Trust accumulates only when:

- continuity holds across time
- replay holds across the accumulation interval
- validation holds across the accumulation interval
- authority scope is unchanged or transitioned explicitly
- unsupported surfaces remain explicit
- failure boundaries remain explicit
- pressure events are recorded, not omitted

Trust accumulation is slow, mechanical, pressure-tested, and resettable. It
is not accelerated by capability, branding, adoption, or external endorsement.
None of those quantities are admissible inputs to accumulation.

---

## 12. Trust Boundary Rules

A trust position is bounded by:

- the declared scope under which it accumulated
- the implementations under which it accumulated
- the languages under which it accumulated
- the failure-class taxonomy under which it accumulated
- the canonicalization surfaces under which it accumulated
- the release set under which it accumulated

Trust does not silently extend across these boundaries. A surface that was
not present during accumulation is not covered by the accumulated trust. A
claim that the accumulated trust covers such a surface is a trust failure.

---

## 13. Trust Failure Rules

A trust failure must be:

- explicit
- classified
- recorded
- non-silent
- non-recoverable without reset, partial reset, or re-accumulation

A trust failure must not be converted into a warning. It must not be averaged
against prior successes. It must not be aggregated into a softer narrative
of overall reliability. Failure rewriting is operationally equivalent to
forgery.

---

## 14. Trust Invalidation Rules

A trust position is invalidated when any of the following hold:

- the underlying records are no longer replayable
- the validation artifacts are no longer reconstructable
- the declared authority scope was silently broadened
- the canonicalization surface changed without recorded transition
- the validator behavior changed without recorded transition
- a release in the accumulation interval is replay-invalid
- a dependency formed without evidence
- operational state is hidden from the record

Invalidation is not optional. An invalidated trust position must not be
relied upon as an accumulated position.

---

## 15. Trust Drift

Trust drift is the unrecorded shift in any of the surfaces against which
trust was accumulated. Drift is forbidden in any form that is not:

- explicit
- versioned
- recorded
- replayable against prior records

A surface that drifts silently invalidates the trust accumulated against it,
to the point of drift, regardless of subsequent behavior. Drift is not
neutralized by later good behavior.

---

## 16. Trust Under Pressure

Trust accumulates only under recorded pressure. Pressure includes, at minimum:

- replay against adversarial inputs
- validation against malformed records
- authority tests against scope-violating actions
- workflow continuity tests across failures
- canonicalization tests across implementations and languages
- release tests against external reconstruction

Pressure events that are absent from the record do not contribute to
accumulation. Trust accumulated in the absence of pressure is not operational
trust. It is comfort, and comfort is not admissible.

---

## 17. Trust Across Versions

When a governed surface changes versions, the following must hold:

- prior records remain replayable against the prior version
- the new version's scope is explicitly declared
- the new version's failure boundaries are explicitly declared
- behavioral differences from the prior version are explicit
- prior trust positions do not silently inherit forward

A version transition is a trust boundary. Trust does not cross a boundary
implicitly. Trust crosses only by explicit, recorded continuation against an
intact substrate, or it does not cross at all.

---

## 18. Trust Across Releases

A release is a trust-bearing artifact only when it is:

- replayable from the released record
- validated against a reconstructable validation artifact
- consistent across its declared implementations and languages
- explicit about unsupported surfaces
- explicit about failure boundaries

A release that fails any of these conditions does not contribute to trust
accumulation. It interrupts it, and the interruption is itself a recorded
event.

---

## 19. Trust Across Implementations

When multiple implementations of a governed surface exist, trust accumulates
only across implementations that produce identical inspectable output for
fixed inputs.

An implementation that diverges in inspectable output does not share the
trust position of the implementations from which it diverged. The divergence
is a separate trust position with a separate accumulation interval.

Cross-implementation trust claims that exceed the cross-implementation
evidence are forbidden.

---

## 20. Trust Across Time

Trust accumulates against time-stamped, ordered records. The ordering must be
preserved. The interval must be recorded. The pressure events within the
interval must be recorded.

A trust position whose interval cannot be reconstructed is not a trust
position. It is a current snapshot. Snapshots do not accumulate.

Long intervals do not produce trust on their own. An empty interval — one
without pressure, replay, validation, or recorded continuity — accumulates
nothing, regardless of duration.

---

## 21. Trust Claims

A trust claim is admissible only when it is bounded by:

- the surfaces under which trust accumulated
- the interval over which trust accumulated
- the pressure events recorded in the interval
- the authority scope under which trust accumulated
- the unsupported surfaces explicitly declared

A trust claim that exceeds any of these bounds is not a trust claim. It is
an assertion that the system has not earned, and assertions of unearned
trust are themselves trust failures.

---

## 22. Forbidden Trust Claims

The following claims are forbidden unless each is backed by mechanically
reconstructable evidence:

- trust beyond evidence
- safety beyond declared threat boundaries
- replay beyond replay proof
- interoperability beyond cross-language equivalence evidence
- authority beyond declared scope
- adoption beyond recorded dependency evidence
- deterministic claims without reproducibility evidence
- continuity claims without record continuity
- pressure-tested claims without pressure records
- AGI claims
- consciousness claims

A forbidden trust claim that appears in or alongside a governed artifact does
not merely weaken the artifact. It invalidates the trust position the
artifact would otherwise contribute to, because a trust position that
contains an unverifiable claim is itself unverifiable.

---

## 23. Trust Failure Classes

The following failure classes are normative and must be used uniformly across
governed surfaces.

- **TR-1 Silent Drift.** A surface against which trust accumulated has shifted
  without explicit, recorded transition.
- **TR-2 Replay Failure.** A record in the accumulation interval no longer
  reproduces its prior outcome.
- **TR-3 Hidden Authority Expansion.** Operational authority extended beyond
  the scope under which trust accumulated, without explicit transition.
- **TR-4 Validation Drift.** Validator behavior shifted within the
  accumulation interval without explicit, recorded transition.
- **TR-5 Audit Discontinuity.** The record continuity required by the
  accumulation interval is broken.
- **TR-6 Canonicalization Drift.** A canonicalization surface invoked during
  accumulation changed without explicit, recorded transition.
- **TR-7 Unsupported Claim Surface.** A trust claim was emitted about a
  surface not present in the accumulation evidence.
- **TR-8 Release Inconsistency.** A release in the accumulation interval is
  replay-invalid or cross-implementation inconsistent.
- **TR-9 Dependency Without Evidence.** Operational dependency formed against
  a surface without replay, validation, or recorded scope.
- **TR-10 Hidden Operational State.** Operational behavior depends on state
  that is not present in the record.

These classes are not exhaustive of all conceivable defects, but they are the
minimum taxonomy required for cross-implementation consistency.

---

## 24. Trust Recovery Rules

Trust recovery, where permissible, must be:

- explicit
- scoped to a recorded recovery interval
- preceded by an explicit failure record
- preceded by remediation that is itself reconstructable
- bounded by the surfaces under which the failure occurred

Recovery does not erase the failure. Recovery resumes accumulation against an
intact substrate, beginning from the recovery point. Prior accumulation
across the failure interval is not retroactively repaired.

---

## 25. Trust Reset Conditions

A trust reset is required when any of the following hold:

- the underlying records are unrecoverable
- the validation surface is unrecoverable
- the authority scope was redefined silently
- the canonicalization surface was redefined silently
- a release in the interval is permanently replay-invalid
- the audit continuity cannot be reconstructed
- hidden operational state is discovered

A reset returns the accumulation interval to zero. Prior trust does not carry
across a reset. A reset is itself a recorded event with explicit cause.

---

## 26. Trust And Public Evidence

A trust position is operationally meaningful only when its evidence is
externally inspectable. Internal confidence is not a trust position. Internal
review is not a trust position. Internal dashboards are not a trust position.

Public evidence must include, at minimum:

- replayable releases
- reconstructable validation artifacts
- declared authority scope
- declared unsupported surfaces
- declared failure boundaries
- the accumulation interval

A system whose trust evidence cannot be inspected by an external party
without privileged access has no externally admissible trust position,
regardless of internal belief.

---

## 27. Long-Term Trust Continuity

Trust continuity must hold across:

- time
- versions
- releases
- machines
- implementations
- languages
- operators
- organizations

Continuity does not require behavioral stasis. It requires that every change
in the surfaces against which trust accumulated is explicit, recorded, and
replayable against the prior record. A surface that loses continuity loses
its contribution to the trust position retroactively to the point of loss.

---

## 28. What Must Never Change

The following are invariant under all future revisions of this law and all
future revisions of any governed surface bound by it.

- operational trust is accumulated, not granted
- trust requires replayability
- trust requires validated continuity
- trust requires bounded authority
- trust requires explicit unsupported surfaces
- trust requires explicit failure boundaries
- trust requires recorded pressure
- trust does not transfer across silent boundary changes
- trust resets when its substrate is invalidated
- forbidden trust claims invalidate the artifacts that contain them

These are the load-bearing invariants. They do not bend.

---

## 29. What May Evolve

The following may evolve under explicit, versioned, recorded transitions:

- the failure-class taxonomy beyond the TR-1..TR-10 minimum
- the public-evidence schema, provided prior evidence remains replayable
- the recorded pressure-event taxonomy
- the declared accumulation-interval representation
- the recovery and reset record schemas
- the cross-implementation and cross-language conformance surface, under
  existing canon

Evolution is not drift. Evolution is recorded, declared, and replayable.
Drift is none of those.

---

## 30. Final Law

A governed system does not request trust. It accumulates operational
credibility through replayable continuity.

Trust law exists to convert operational dependency from belief into
reconstructable evidence.

A trust position that cannot be replayed from the record is not a trust
position. A trust claim that exceeds its evidence is not a trust claim. A
dependency that forms without evidence is not a dependency. It is a wager.

Beyond the boundary of reconstructable evidence, operational trust does not
exist.

---

## Appendix A. What Destroys Operational Trust?

The following conditions destroy operational trust, each independently and
without compounding:

- silent drift
- unverifiable claims
- replay-invalid releases
- hidden authority expansion
- hidden validator mutation
- undocumented canonicalization change
- release inconsistency across implementations or languages
- unsupported interoperability claims
- audit discontinuity
- hidden operational state

Any one of these conditions is sufficient to invalidate the trust position
to which it is adjacent. They do not average. They each independently
terminate accumulation.

---

## Appendix B. What Makes Trust Operationally Legitimate?

The following conditions are jointly necessary for operational legitimacy:

- deterministic continuity
- replayable releases
- explicit unsupported surfaces
- reconstructable governance
- pressure-tested behavior across recorded intervals
- validated enforcement
- bounded claims
- evidence-backed operation

Partial satisfaction does not yield partial legitimacy. A trust position is
either reconstructable from the record under these conditions, or it is not.

---

## Appendix C. What Trust Claims Are Forbidden?

The following claims are forbidden unless each is backed by mechanically
reconstructable evidence:

- trust beyond evidence
- safety beyond declared threat boundaries
- replay beyond replay proof
- interoperability beyond cross-language equivalence evidence
- authority beyond declared scope
- adoption beyond recorded dependency evidence
- deterministic claims without reproducibility evidence
- continuity claims without record continuity
- pressure-tested claims without pressure records
- AGI claims
- consciousness claims

A forbidden trust claim does not merely weaken the artifact in which it
appears. It invalidates the trust position that artifact would otherwise
contribute to, because a position that contains an unverifiable claim is
itself unverifiable.

---

## Appendix D. What Happens Without Trust Law?

In the absence of trust law, the following outcomes are not hypothetical.
They are the steady-state result.

- governance collapse — every governance act becomes a request for belief
- operational drift — surfaces shift without record, and dependents do not
  know
- unverifiable dependency — operational reliance forms against assertion
  rather than evidence
- institutional distrust — external parties cannot reconstruct why the system
  should be relied upon
- release ambiguity — releases circulate without bounded trust positions
- audit collapse — accumulation intervals cannot be reconstructed
- credibility erosion — accumulated reputation collapses to its evidentiary
  floor, which without trust law is zero

Trust law is the surface at which operational dependency stops being a
matter of belief and becomes a matter of record. Beyond that surface,
operational trust does not exist.
